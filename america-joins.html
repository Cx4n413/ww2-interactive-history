A Date Which Will Live in Infamy

December 7, 1941

The Path to War

For years, the United States maintained a policy of isolationism while supporting the Allies through the Lend-Lease Act. However, rising tensions with Imperial Japan over embargoes in the Pacific set the stage for a dramatic shift.

Morning of Dec 7, 1941

The Attack on Pearl Harbor

In a surprise military strike, the Imperial Japanese Navy Air Service attacked the United States naval base at Pearl Harbor in Honolulu, Hawaii. The attack killed 2,403 U.S. personnel and destroyed or damaged 19 U.S. Navy ships.

Dec 8, 1941

Declaration of War

President Franklin D. Roosevelt addressed Congress, calling December 7 "a date which will live in infamy." Congress officially declared war on the Empire of Japan, officially bringing the U.S. into World War II.

Dec 11, 1941

The Conflict Expands

Honoring their Tripartite Pact with Japan, Germany and Italy declared war on the United States. The U.S. responded in kind, fully engaging in the global conflict across both the European and Pacific theaters.

1942 and Beyond

Mobilization and Industry

The U.S. economy transformed entirely. Factories shifted from producing consumer goods to building tanks, planes, and ships. Millions of Americans, including women (epitomized by Rosie the Riveter), entered the workforce to support the war effort.
